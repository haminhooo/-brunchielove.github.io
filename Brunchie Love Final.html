A Brief History of Brunch

A quick breakdown of how our favorite kind of early afternoon eating came to be.

  • 1895

    “Brunch” Becomes a Word

    ​What does seem certain is that the word "brunch"—that playful blend of "breakfast" and "lunch"—first appeared in print in an 1895 Hunter's Weekly article. In "Brunch: A Plea," British author Guy Beringer suggested an alternative to the heavy, post-church Sunday meals in favor of lighter fare served late in the morning. ''Brunch is cheerful, sociable and inciting,'' Beringer says. ''It is talk-compelling. It puts you in a good temper, it makes you satisfied with yourself and your fellow beings, it sweeps away the worries and cobwebs of the week.''

  • 1930s

    Brunch goes to America

    Brunch hit big in America in the 1930’s via actors making their way across the country from New York to California by train. During these publicity tours for their upcoming movies, they would stop in Chicago for this beloved meal. It’s midday timing allowed them to sleep later, which was an especially exciting selling point. Maybe this is why George Clooney is still such a big fan. Brunch’s biggest push, however came during a massive moment of progress for women in America. When women entered the workforce, they needed a break from cooking and pushed the family to start going out for a meal on the day they would generally sit around the table as a family – Sunday.

  • 2000’s

    Brunch Goes Global

    Chinese dim sum sees a rise in popularity, and pretty much any cuisine gets an egg slapped on top of it. Some people start lining up for brunch hotspots, while others roll their eyes and take a stance on brunch’s socio-economic implications.

  • Today

    Brunch Hulks Out

    Brunching has become a weekend activity worthy of classification of ‘sport’. Lineups are long, plates are piled high, syrup is infused, waffles are sandwiches, cocktails come in pitchers – it’s a little crazy. But because of the value of flipping tables, brunch has become more rushed than leisurely. Though one constant has remained the same: it’s still a social thing. Thanks to social media and foodie culture, it’s also about sharing it with the world.

Brunches all over the world

People all over the world eat brunch, but the food they eat differs from country to country.

Dimsum - Hong Kong

Steamed and fried dumpblings make dim sum a real crown-pleaser. Fillings commonly include seafood, pork and vegetables. For something more exotic, try the chicken feet.

Oeufs en Cocotte au Saumon Fumé - France

A small yet mighty bake of eggs, crème fraîche, smoked salmon and garlic. Not complete without a hunk of crusty French baguette, obviously.

Ackee and Saltfish - Jamaica

A traditional Jamaican dish combining cooked ackee fruit and scotch bonnet chilies. The result is a sweet yet spicy dish that's often served with sides such as boiled plantain, breadfruit and, on occasion, rice and peas.

Nasi Goreng - Malaysia

One of Malaysia's most enduring dishes. Nasi goreng blends traditional flavors such as sambal belachan (chili paste) and kecap manis (Indonesian sweet soy sauce), with stir-fried rice, eggs, meat, garlic and onion.

Huevos divorciados - Mexico

Not for the faint-hearted, this fiery dish combines green and red salsas over two fried eggs. An accompaniment of tortilla chips and, sometimes, refried beans and cotija cheese give it an unmistakable Mexican flavor.

Egg Benedict - America

Warm, toasted English muffins are the base for crispy bacon, poached eggs and a generous drizzle of sharp, zesty hollandaise sauce.

What Milanese each for brunch? And where?

Half breakfast, half lunch, straight from the Anglo-Saxon word, brunch is now the coolest meal. In Italy too this habit is growing more and more popular, becoming a real trend. In Milan, an international city always up-to-date with new trends from abroad, the places for the weekend brunch are multiplying. And you have many offers: from the most traditional American plates to the typically Italian ones. Here you will find a guide to the best brunch in Milan, suitable for couples, groups of friends or families, for every taste and need.

L’ov Milano

13€ - 23€

Viale Premuda, 14

  • Menu on Saturdays, Sundays and public holidays.
  • Favorite brunch food: Pancakes, sandwiches, and burgers (as well as delicious desserts)
  • Recommended: try something off their round-the-clock egg menu

RITA & COCKTAILS

8€ - 16€

Via Angelo Fumagalli, 1

  • American omelets, sausages, and pancakes
  • Asian-infused offerings: Sushi Rice Burger, Mr. Prawn Roll
  • Finish your brunch off the proper way with a slice of NY Cheesecake or their iconic Chocolate Brownies

California Bakery

8€ - 12€

Piazza Sant’Eustorgio, 4

  • One of the most exhaustive brunch menus in Milan
  • Brunch on Saturdays and Sundays
  • Scrambled eggs, New York-style bagels, a big hamburger, or a salad (hangover cure)

God saved the food

10€ - 18€

Via Tortona, 34

  • The eggs are definitely a highlight
  • A range of Asian-inspired dishes masterfully prepared in their Wok
  • Always leaves you satisfied

oTTo

6€ - 8€

Via Paolo Sarpi, 8

  • Every Saturday and Sunday
  • Famous “Quadrotti" a box of bread slices topped with unique combinations, accompanied by fruit, yogurt, potatoes, and coffee
  • The most Instagrammable brunch in the city
